Pillar 1: Cognitive Biases

The Sunk Cost Fallacy: How to Cut Your Losses and Move On

The irresistible urge to finish something that is already failing, driven only by the resources (time, money, effort) already invested.

Core Statement: A sunk cost is any cost that has already been incurred and cannot be recovered. Rational decision-making must only consider future costs and future benefits, completely ignoring past investments.

This fallacy prevents countless projects from being killed, keeps people trapped in dead-end careers, and forces us to finish bad books or films. It is the feeling that "I've come this far, I can't stop now," even when stopping is the most logical choice.

Why We Fall for the Sunk Cost

The fallacy is emotionally compelling, primarily due to two psychological drivers:

1. Loss Aversion (The Pain of Waste)

We are psychologically wired to feel the pain of a loss (the sunk cost) much more intensely than the pleasure of an equivalent gain (the future opportunity). Killing the project feels like admitting the past time/money was *wasted*, which is painful. Continuing it offers the illusion of recovery.

2. Self-Justification (Protecting the Ego)

Admitting a mistake—that the initial investment decision was wrong—hurts the ego. By doubling down, we try to retroactively justify the past actions, hoping a miracle turnaround will validate our initial choice. The cost of admitting failure often feels greater than the cost of continuing to fail.

Toolkit: How to Break the Cycle

These techniques re-frame the problem to isolate the past from the future, enabling rational decisions.

1. The "Zero Budget" Rule

For a failing endeavor, ask yourself: "If I had zero invested in this right now, would I choose to start it today?" If the answer is no, stop immediately. The past investment is irrelevant to the future decision.

2. Separate the Deciders

If you were the person who initiated the project, appoint a new, objective person (or use a friend/colleague) to make the cut-off decision. They have no ego tied to the sunk costs and can judge the future potential more clearly.

3. Define the Opportunity Cost

Instead of focusing on the $5,000 lost, focus on what you could do with the time/money you're saving by quitting. Quitting project A doesn't mean failure, it means successfully starting project B. Articulate the positive new endeavor that the freed resources will enable.

The only currency that matters is the future. Past costs are non-recoverable tuition paid to the school of experience.
